## Deeper Look at C-Reactive Protein (CRP)

CRP levels showed a significant reduction in the latest blood test. In 2020, CRP measurements ranged from 0.3 to 1.01 mg/L. The latest measurement is 0.27 mg/L, which is about half of the average 2020 value (0.54 mg/L). Keeping CRP low is important as it increases with aging and higher levels are associated with increased all-cause mortality risk.

### Variables Impacting CRP

**1. Blood Cholesterol and CRP:**
Higher blood levels of total cholesterol (TC) were correlated with lower CRP, within a personal range of 133 to 185 mg/dL. This suggests a potential inverse relationship.

**2. Dietary Cholesterol and Blood Cholesterol:**
Higher average dietary cholesterol intake was correlated with higher blood TC levels.

**3. Dietary Cholesterol and CRP:**
Dietary cholesterol was not significantly correlated with CRP directly. This suggests an indirect effect:
Dietary Cholesterol (higher) -> Blood Total Cholesterol (higher) -> (Undetermined Mechanism) -> CRP (lower)

**4. Components of Total Cholesterol (TC) and CRP:**
TC = HDL + LDL + VLDL. Investigating individual components:
*   **VLDL:** Not correlated with CRP.
*   **LDL:** Relatively close to a significant correlation with CRP (p=0.1).
*   **HDL:** Higher HDL was inversely correlated with lower CRP (statistically significant).

Thus, the pathway appears to be:
Dietary Cholesterol (higher) -> Blood Total Cholesterol (higher) -> HDL (higher) -> CRP (lower)

### Factors Impacting HDL

**1. Dietary Cholesterol and HDL:**
Dietary cholesterol was not significantly correlated with HDL.

**2. Sodium and Fructose Intake and HDL:**
*   **Sodium:** Higher average daily sodium intake (within a range of 1400-2600 mg/day) was correlated with higher HDL.
*   **Fructose:** Higher average daily total fructose intake (including fructose from sucrose) was correlated with lower HDL.

A multivariate model including sodium and fructose explained 40% of the variability in HDL levels. This leaves 60% of HDL variability to be explained by other factors, which will be explored in future analyses.

### Sodium, Fructose, and CRP

**1. Sodium and CRP:**
Higher sodium intake (within a range of 1500-2500 mg/day) was correlated with lower CRP (p=0.05).

**2. Fructose and CRP:**
Average daily fructose intake was not correlated with CRP.

Thus, the pathways identified are:
Sodium (higher) -> HDL (higher) -> CRP (lower)
Fructose (higher) -> HDL (lower) -> CRP (higher)

## Future Interventions and Goals

For the next blood test, the following dietary adjustments will be experimented with:
*   **Increase Dietary Cholesterol:** Aim to increase daily intake closer to 250 mg/day (from the current 178 mg/day). Dietary cholesterol intakes up to 250 mg/day are associated with the lowest all-cause mortality risk.
*   **Increase Sodium:** Experiment with a slightly higher intake, potentially around 2700 mg/day, while closely monitoring blood pressure, creatinine, and glucose levels to avoid adverse effects.
*   **Limit Fructose:** Target an average daily fructose intake of 70-80 grams or less.

The hypothesis is that these changes will further reduce CRP and increase HDL.
