## Importance of NAD

NAD is a vital molecule, often referred to as the "supernova of molecules," because it is involved in the function of over 500 other enzymes. Without NAD, life is nearly impossible. Its most critical function is the production of cellular energy (ATP) primarily in the mitochondria. Without sufficient NAD, cells cannot produce ATP, leading to a lack of energy for bodily functions.

Beyond ATP production, NAD supports numerous other functions across every cell, tissue, and organ in the body. It is crucial for muscle function, brain function (which consumes over 20% of the body's energy despite being only 2% of body weight), and many other cellular processes.

## NAD Decline with Age

NAD levels unfortunately decline with age. Data from tens of thousands of NAD tests reveal that this decline is rapid and begins surprisingly early:
*   In teenagers, 75% have NAD levels above 40 micromolar, but about 25% are already suboptimal or deficient.
*   By the late twenties/early thirties, approximately 75% of individuals are suboptimal or deficient in NAD.
*   In individuals in their forties, fifties, and sixties and beyond, NAD levels are only about 30-50% of what is typically seen in very young children.

This decline in NAD levels is associated with various health issues and the overall aging process.

## Strategies to Elevate NAD

There are four main categories of strategies to increase NAD levels or slow their degradation:
1.  **Inhibit NAD Degrading Enzymes:** NAD is degraded by enzymes such as CD38 and those involved in DNA repair. Inhibiting these enzymes could theoretically slow NAD degradation and increase levels, but this strategy is practically very difficult.
2.  **Increase NAD Synthesizing Enzymes:** Promoting the activity of enzymes like NAMPT and NMAT, which are key in NAD biosynthesis, is an important strategy.
3.  **Direct NAD+ Administration:** NAD IV (intravenous) has been used for decades to treat various neurological and infectious diseases. However, its efficacy for general longevity and wellness optimization is a subject of ongoing study.
4.  **NAD Precursors:** Providing the building blocks that cells use to make NAD is a common strategy. There are at least five or six known precursors.

## NAD Administration Methods: IV and Subcutaneous Injections

### NAD IV Efficacy
*   While NAD IV has clinical efficacy for specific neurological and infectious conditions, its impact on intracellular NAD levels for general longevity and wellness is questioned.
*   A clinical trial involving 10 individuals receiving five sessions of NAD IV (750 mg NAD+ per session) showed **no change in intracellular NAD levels**.
*   When these same individuals were subsequently given oral Vitality Boost, their NAD levels significantly increased, with most reaching above 40 micromolar. This suggests oral supplements can be more effective at increasing intracellular NAD.

### Subcutaneous NAD Injections
*   Subcutaneous NAD injections do increase NAD levels, unlike NAD IV, possibly due to slower degradation outside the bloodstream, allowing more time for cellular absorption.
*   However, the levels achieved are often not high enough, typically remaining in the 30s micromolar range for tested individuals.
*   Similar to the IV study, subsequent oral supplementation with Vitality Boost proved to be much more effective than subcutaneous injections in raising NAD levels.

## Impact of Diet, Exercise, and Inflammation on NAD

### Diet and Exercise Limitations
It is often assumed that diet and exercise can significantly increase NAD levels. However:
*   While some exercise and diet interventions may increase NAD, the increase is typically small (e.g., 10-20%), which is often insufficient to make a noticeable difference in health or performance for most individuals who need to double or triple their NAD levels.
*   For about 90% of individuals, the primary deficit is in the supply of NAD precursors, while 10% may have deficiencies in NAD-synthesizing enzymes or precursor absorption.
*   Paradoxically, individuals who exercise intensely or overtrain (e.g., a young boxing champion with NAD levels as low as 19.1 micromolar) can have lower NAD levels, potentially due to increased inflammation. Inflammation is a major factor in reduced NAD levels.

### Gut Microbiome Role
Microorganisms in the gut utilize NAD in their metabolic processes and may contribute to NAD levels in the body, but the extent of this impact and the feasibility of manipulating the microbiome to increase NAD are currently unknown.

### Tryptophan as a Precursor
Tryptophan is an NAD precursor but requires many chemical steps for conversion. It has been found to be inefficient in increasing NAD levels. One individual experienced severe gastrointestinal pain at 2 grams/day of tryptophan, with no observable increase in NAD.

### Senescence-Associated Beta-Galactosidase (SA-beta-gal)
SA-beta-gal is identified as the best single biomarker for senescence, showing increases in individuals with various diseases and correlating with patient response to therapy and survival in studies (e.g., cancer patients).

### Clotho Protein
Clotho is an important and responsive biomarker for overall health.
*   It is often deficient in individuals with kidney problems (though not 100% of cases).
*   Levels are also severely deficient in a significant proportion of cancer and Alzheimer's patients.
*   Clotho levels can precipitously drop after infections like COVID.
*   Its responsiveness to changing health conditions makes it valuable for detecting hidden health issues.

### Kidney Function Biomarkers
*   **Creatinine:** While an increase can indicate declining kidney function, it's also affected by muscle mass and creatine supplementation. Biohackers taking creatine often see elevated creatinine, which can cause confusion if not interpreted alongside other markers.
*   **Cystatin C:** Measured alongside creatinine to estimate Glomerular Filtration Rate (eGFR), providing a more accurate assessment of kidney function, especially in individuals with altered muscle mass or creatine intake.

### Ergothioneine
Ergothioneine is a metabolite whose levels decline with age. Rich sources include mushrooms. There are currently no widely available commercial tests for ergothioneine, but Genfinity is exploring the development of such a test and has observed improvements in other biomarkers in individuals supplementing with it.

### Quinolinate
Quinolinate is a neurotoxic metabolite that increases with aging. While there is interest in measuring toxins and microplastics, the challenge lies in actionability and cost-effective intervention (e.g., plasma apheresis is very expensive). However, through a 'test, act, optimize' approach, individuals could track quinolinate levels in conjunction with diet, exercise, and supplements to identify personal strategies for keeping levels low.
