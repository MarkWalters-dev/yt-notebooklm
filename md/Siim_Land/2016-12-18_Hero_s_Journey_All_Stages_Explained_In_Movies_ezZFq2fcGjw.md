# 2016-12-18 Hero s Journey All Stages Explained In Movies

## Executive Summary

*   The Hero's Journey, or monomyth, is a universal narrative structure identified by Joseph Campbell, depicting a hero's transformative adventure into a special world and eventual return.
*   Comprising 17 stages across three acts—Departure, Initiation, and Return—the journey details the hero's psychological and external growth, from an ordinary individual to a transformed being.
*   Key stages involve the hero receiving a 'call to adventure,' refusing it, meeting a mentor, facing trials and temptations, confronting a source of ultimate power (atonement), undergoing a spiritual death and rebirth (apotheosis), and achieving a 'boon.'
*   The journey is a metaphor for personal maturation and self-actualization, where individuals confront inner 'shadow' traits, overcome fears, and develop their full potential.
*   Ultimately, the hero achieves mastery of two worlds (ordinary and special), leading to freedom from limiting beliefs and the ability to live authentically and bestow wisdom upon others.

## Introduction to the Hero's Journey

The concept of the hero's journey, introduced by Joseph Campbell, is a universal monomyth found across cultures. It involves a hero who ventures on an adventure into a special world, faces a crisis, emerges as a victor, and returns home as a transformed being. This self-imposed narrative helps define one's place in the cosmos and facilitates individual maturation, bringing greater meaning to life than ordinary myths. The protagonist, initially a lesser version of themselves, is "the man of self-achieved submission" who is shaped into a "superhero within" during trials and tribulations. The journey involves massive internal and external transformations, shedding underdeveloped traits and growing into a greater version of oneself through epiphanies and development.

Campbell described the monomyth in 17 stages, which he termed 'mythims,' bundled together into a structured narrative. The 17 stages are divided into three acts:
*   **Act One:** Departure or separation from the ordinary world.
*   **Act Two:** Descent into and initiation inside the special world.
*   **Act Three:** Return back to the ordinary world.

This pattern involves a separation from the world, a penetration to some source of power, and a life-enhancing return.

## The 17 Stages of the Hero's Journey

The journey's progression can be visualized as:
Ordinary World -> Call to Adventure -> Refusal of the Call -> Meeting the Mentor -> Crossing the First Threshold -> Road of Trials -> Temptation -> Atonement of the Father -> Apotheosis -> The Ultimate Boon -> Refusal of the Return -> The Magic Flight -> Rescue from Without -> Crossing the Return Threshold -> Master of the Two Worlds -> Freedom to Live.

### Act One: Departure/Separation from the Ordinary World

### Stage 1: The Ordinary World
This is the "before the beginning," setting the tone for the entire journey. It explains the backstory, principles of existence in the given world, and foreshadows both the ordinary and special worlds, creating contrast. Dramatic questions, such as what constitutes good and evil, are often raised. The hero is introduced in their everyday setting, revealing their traits, flaws, and desires. An example is the opening scenes of *The Lord of the Rings*, narrating the history of the One Ring.

### Stage 2: The Call to Adventure
A trigger—a message, a catalyst—gets the story rolling. This call to action puts a direct threat on the hero's safety, family, community peace, or poses a temptation like hidden gold or glory. The ordinary world becomes unstable, needing risky action to restore balance. The purpose of the call is to alleviate a lack or need, such as a kidnapping, necessity for survival, or the hero's growth. In *The Hobbit*, Gandalf's arrival and the dwarves crashing Bilbo's place serves as the call.

### Stage 3: Refusal of the Call
The hero's immediate reaction is to refuse the call, perceiving the adventure as too dangerous and challenging. The hero is comfortable in their current setting and makes excuses. For instance, Bilbo initially resists the idea of dragons, and Neo initially disbelieves Trinity about the Matrix. This refusal is an attempt to delay an inevitable fate and can occur at later stages, as seen in *The Lord of the Rings* when Sam initially resists protecting Frodo, or when Frodo realizes he must take the ring to Mordor himself. This mirrors real-life hesitancy to pursue dreams due to fear, waiting for a "perfect moment" that never arrives.

### Stage 4: Meeting the Mentor
At a critical point, the hero is forced to accept the call due to a dire need for deliverance. This doesn't necessarily mean a physical adventure; it can be a realization of the need for personal change. The hero meets a mentor who provides something essential: an object of importance (like the One Ring), information, wise advice, practical training, or a boost of self-confidence. The mentor dispels doubts and fears, providing strength and courage to begin the quest.

### Stage 5: Crossing the First Threshold
The hero is now ready to begin their quest, whether physical, mental, or spiritual. Despite initial efforts to resist, they cross the first threshold into the unknown world. This is illustrated when Sam, in *The Lord of the Rings*, takes a step beyond the furthest he's ever been from home. Neo taking the red pill and exiting the Matrix into the real world is another example.

There is often a **Threshold Guardian** protecting the path. This archetype symbolizes an illusory threat—internal doubt, external enemies, or a trickster—that tests the hero's wit and skill. The transcript also includes **The Belly of the Whale** as an additional component here, representing the final separation from the hero's known world and self, where there is no turning back. For the Hobbits, this was reaching the town of Bree, surrounded by uncertainty.

### Act Two: Initiation in the Special World

#### Act Two A: Descent

### Stage 6: The Road of Trials
Having committed to the journey, the hero undergoes a series of trials and tribulations. Antagonistic forces will set traps and introduce enemies, but the hero also gains new friends and allies. This stage tests the hero's skills and power, providing deeper insight into their character with each challenge. Failure in some trials can lead to wisdom about the special world's rules and learning from mistakes. Examples include the children at Hogwarts facing troubles that make them better wizards, and the development of alliances like Princess Fiona and the ogre, Jake Sully and Neytiri, or Peter Parker and Mary Jane.

#### Act Two B: Initiation

### Stage 8: Woman as Temptress
The hero faces distractions and temptations that could lead them astray, often physical pleasures such as money, fame, food, or sex. The "woman" is a metaphor for life's temptations, testing the hero's ability to resist immediate gratification and endure discomfort, remaining committed to their call. This requires perseverance and grit. Odysseus's encounter with the Sirens, where he had his crew tie him to the mast while they filled their ears with beeswax, exemplifies using wit to overcome this challenge.

### Stage 9: Atonement of the Father
Here, the hero confronts the ultimate source of power in their life, often a literal or metaphorical father figure. This is the central point of the journey, representing a maturation where the hero gathers the courage to become independent and confronts the greatest authority. Luke Skywalker's confrontation with Darth Vader, where he discovers Vader is his father, is a literal example, leading to a massive revelation. The father represents power over the hero, which the hero must overcome to become seemingly omnipotent. This stage can also involve confronting the dark parts of oneself, the 'shadow,' as seen in Spider-Man when Peter Parker fights the symbiotic Venom poisoning his mind.

### Stage 10: Apotheosis
Translated from Greek as "to deify," apotheosis is the hero's transcendence to a higher plane of existence through a symbolic death and resurrection. The hero realizes the divine within themselves, shedding all doubts and fully embracing their abilities, becoming unstoppable. Neo in *The Matrix*, shot by Agent Smith, is reborn through Trinity's love, experiencing a cathartic breakthrough. Similarly, Gandalf's transformation from Gandalf the Grey to Gandalf the White after battling the Balrog signifies gaining true access to infinite potential. Joseph Campbell describes this as those who "know not only that the everlasting lies in them, but that what they and all things really are is the everlasting."

### Stage 11: The Ultimate Boon
This is the highest achievement of the quest. The hero has emerged victoriously from the climax and has apotheosized into the divine superhero they are destined to be. Examples include Luke trusting the Force to destroy the Death Star, the One Ring being cast into Mount Doom, and Aragorn taking his place on the throne of Gondor. The ultimate boon represents seizing the "sword" or gaining access to the "elixir of life"—a moment of clarity where the hero understands their true self and place in the grand scheme of things, attaining clairvoyance.

### Act Three: Return to the Ordinary World

### Stage 12: Refusal of the Return
Having reached enlightenment and a supreme mode of being, the hero may not want to return to the mundane ordinary world, finding it meaningless. Frodo, exhausted at Mount Doom, simply wants to give up. This mirrors real-world experiences, such as veterans returning from war who may find everyday life dull after intense experiences.

### Stage 13: The Magic Flight
Sometimes, obtaining the ultimate boon involves a "hot pursuit" if it belongs to the gods or requires a quick escape. The hero might need to steal the boon, leading to an exhilarating chase back towards the ordinary world.

### Stage 14: Rescue from Without
On the verge of collapse, the hero may lack the strength to make it back alone. Allies often ride to the rescue. Frodo and Sam are rescued by Gandalf and the eagles as Mount Doom collapses. Han Solo appears to save Luke by taking out enemy fighters just before he destroys the Death Star.

### Stage 15: Crossing the Return Threshold
The hero crosses back into the ordinary world, ascending from the "underworld" and being symbolically reborn. This threshold is as important as the first, as the hero's initial descent symbolized a death from home life. The challenge is to retain the elixir of life and knowledge gained, bestowing its powers onto others. Odysseus, returning home to Ithaca after many years, disguises himself as a beggar, proves his identity by drawing his bow, and reunites with his loved ones after defeating the suitors.

### Stage 16: Master of the Two Worlds
This stage marks the hero's transformation into a transcendental being capable of moving between the special and ordinary worlds with ease. They have overcome internal and external shadows. Examples include Jesus and Gautama Buddha, Luke becoming a Jedi knight, and Aragorn being crowned king. The hero persists because they choose to, demonstrating mastery.

### Stage 17: Freedom to Live
With self-mastery and discipline, the hero gains the freedom to choose how to live their remaining days. They are powerful, wise, skillful, and liberated from negative aspects of themselves, such as fears, anxiety, and afflictive emotions. They differ from ordinary people still "stuck in the matrix," having seen the true nature of reality and living in the present. Luke becomes one with the Force and teaches others; Neo makes a self-sacrificial choice; the Hobbits become leaders in the Shire. This mastery leads to freedom from the fear of death, which, in turn, is the ultimate freedom to live.

## The Hero's Journey as a Metaphor for Personal Growth

The Hero's Journey represents the maturation process of the human psyche. Act One mirrors childhood dependence, where the hero, like an infant, is safe but vulnerable. The call to adventure signifies the necessity to accept challenges and pursue one's full potential, even if it seems scary.

Act Two involves massive personal development built through challenges, culminating in the atonement and apotheosis. This is the highest point, where the individual symbolically takes power from the greatest source of authority and becomes self-reliant. It moves beyond a victim mentality, allowing the hero to transcend limits.

The Hero's Journey is not just about fighting great evil or perilous adventures; it's a metaphor for overcoming oneself. The initial "hero" symbolizes the lesser, egoistic self with self-imposed doubts. Traversing between the ordinary and divine worlds allows one to transcend limiting beliefs and gain power, transforming into a "superhero version" of oneself with access to true potential.

The call to self-actualization comes from within, an intuitive yearning. Refusing it often stems from fear and limiting beliefs. Decisive action is the "single step" marking the point between the ordinary and special worlds. Embracing this journey, with its inevitable obstacles, challenges, and tricksters, only augments and enhances the hero. External adversity and internal resistance become forces for growth and self-realization.

Perceiving daily challenges as stepping stones to victory, rather than dreadful events, is crucial for successful heroes. Everyone's journey is unique; it requires deep contemplation and self-inquiry to understand one's true calling. Waiting for a "divine revelation" can lead to stagnation. Enlightenment often comes during the hardest challenges in the "innermost cave." Beginning the journey with decisive, steadfast action defines who you are, as stagnation is death, and motion implies true life. Moving towards the self-actualized superhero version of oneself is the ultimate boon of existence.
